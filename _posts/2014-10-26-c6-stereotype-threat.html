Phase 0, Week 6 -

My name is called. I walk to my designated mat number. I glance over at my opponent at the opposite side as I pace back and forth. I'm quivering as I wait to be called to enter. My heart is beating faster and faster. He's younger. He's faster. He's stronger. He's been training longer than I have. I'm nervous. I am scared. I could pass out from a choke. I could be walking away limping. Could be a broken arm. These are some serious risks that I'm taking.

A thousand of these thoughts shoot through my head as anxiously await to fight my first match. I have every reason to fear. I am weaker, I will lose. I am slower, I will lose. I am not as experienced, I will lose. For everything that makes up who I am, I can have every reason to fail. But should it stop me?

The real fight is not with the person in front of me. The real fight is with myself in channeling my energy to "How will I succeed?" rather than focusing on how I will fail.

While this situation and associated feelings are very literal, I also like to use it in the figurative sense and apply it to the other aspects in life. It came to my mind as I learned about "stereotype threat" which is defined as "risk of confirming, as self-characteristic, a negative stereotype about one's group." While my obvious response would be to think of situations about my race, I'd like to take a different approach. A more positive approach. And that approach is to... embrace the risk.

You see, I am comfortable with who I am. And I want to say that I know myself pretty well. I am conscious of how I see myself and how others may see me. I am happy. I am proud of my strengths and I embrace my weaknesses. I understand my disadvantages. I try not to bring race into the picture when thinking of my disadvantages, but in some cases it is inevitable. Again, I ask, should this be enough to stop me?

The approach is very much how I deal with stereotype threats. Accept it, it exists. I am pre-disposed to a certain stereotype. It is who I am belonging to a particular "group". Understand it. Focus on how I will overcome it. Understand the risk.Take the risk. Perhaps I will fail and confirm my stereotype. But should it stop me from giving it my all? If I do not try, I will never know. If I try, there is a chance I may succeed. I can fail and try try again. But, if I never take the risk, there is statistically ZERO chance that I will ever make it.

I finally get called to enter. Bow to the referee and to my opponent as a sign of respect. Am I scared? Yes. But I cannot let the fear stop me from reaching my potential. I must fight the anxiety. I must take the risk to realize the reward. My friends, if we will ever win, we must fight!

"Do not let what you cannot do interfere with what you can do." - John Wooden
